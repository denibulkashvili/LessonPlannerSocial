Lesson Types

Basic Lesson

Generic lesson type that is not limited to a specific lesson structure.

ESA Methodology

ESA is a common methodology that stands for engage, study, and activate and is designed to keep the students interested, motivated, and eager to learn more.

ESA Phases:

  1. Engage phase (Warm-Up): getting the students interested in the class through a game, pictures, audio, video etc.
  2. Study phase: focusing on any aspect of the language, such as grammar or vocabulary and pronunciation and can be done in many different styles, from group examination of a text, to discovery related topic vocabulary, to the teacher giving an explanation of a grammatical pattern.
  3. Activate stage: putting things learned in lesson in action by gettin students to use the language as communicatively as they can, through such activities as discussions, role-play, story building, simulations, debates etc.

Types of ESA Lessons

ESA sraight arrows sequence

Straight arrow best for the beginners because they need guidance of the teacher. ESA Straight Arrows sequence is a type of teaching sequence takes students in a straight line: first the teacher gets the class interested and Engaged, then the students Study something and they then try to Activate it by putting it into practice.

Boomerang sequence

The Boomerang approach after the Engage (E) phase, gets students to perform a task (A) using all and/or any language they know and only then does the teacher go back to the language Study (S). The Study phase is then undertaken based on what the teacher witnessed in the students language performance. The teacher in short will fill in the gaps of the students knowledge. To check that learning has taken place the students are then re-activated (A). It may also be more appropriate for students at intermediate and advanced levels since they have quite a lot of language.

Patchwork sequence

Patchwork lessons include all ESA elements, but can do so more than once and in various orders. A sequence such as E.A.A.S.S.E.S.A would be perfectly possible. EAAASEA Patchwork is commonly used in the class of intermediate and advanced levels, because they probably reflect the way we learn rather chaotically and not always in a straight line.
